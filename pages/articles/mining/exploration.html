Phosphate Exploration Workflow: Target Identification to Feasibility

Location of geophysical surveys carried out in Youssoufia area.

Exploration begins with regional geological synthesis and remote sensing to delineate phosphatic horizons, followed by geophysical surveys (gravity, resistivity, seismic) to map subsurface structure. Targets are tested with systematic core drilling; core logging yields lithology, P₂O₅ grade, thickness and rock quality data. These inputs feed geostatistical and 3-D reserve models used for resource classification and pit design. Simultaneous hydrogeological and environmental baseline programs determine water table depths, recharge dynamics and ecological constraints to inform responsible mine planning.

Regional Mapping & Remote Sensing

Regional mapping and satellite imagery are used to locate outcrops, structural trends and surface alteration that point to phosphatic horizons. High-resolution satellite imagery, aerial photography and geological maps identify surface exposures and structural lineaments. Digital elevation models and multispectral data help distinguish lithologies and identify accessible drill targets. Mapping integrates historic borehole data and field reconnaissance to prioritise survey grids. These mapping products underpin the first-pass resource potential estimates and guide the design of follow-up geophysical programs.

Geophysical Surveys

Geophysical methods (gravity, resistivity, shallow seismic) are essential to define subsurface geometry and to detect water-bearing units that influence mine design. Gravity surveys map density contrasts associated with phosphatic horizons; electrical resistivity and induced polarisation detect conductive clay or water-filled layers. Shallow seismic profiles reveal bedding geometry and faulting. Geophysics reduces drilling uncertainty and helps outline areas of “disturbance” (hard lenses or clay pods) that could complicate extraction. Data are integrated with borehole logs to refine 3-D geological models and hydrogeological risk maps.

Core Drilling & Sampling

Core drilling provides the hard data—rock samples and continuous logs—that confirm grade, thickness and rock quality for reserve estimation. Drilling programs follow grid or fence patterns, with core logging recording lithology, mineralization, structural features and water inflow. Representative core samples are sent to assaying labs for P₂O₅ and impurity analyses. Drilling density is increased in higher-confidence resource categories; results populate borehole databases used for variography and geostatistics. Chain-of-custody and QA/QC (standards, blanks, duplicates) are enforced to ensure sample reliability for reserve reporting.

Hydrogeological & Environmental Baseline

Understanding groundwater and the environmental baseline is critical before any pit is advanced—both for permitting and for practical dewatering design. Hydrogeological programs install piezometers, run pumping tests and measure aquifer transmissivity and hydraulic gradients. Results determine dewatering needs, potential inflows to pits and impacts on nearby water users. Environmental baseline studies measure existing water quality, terrestrial and aquatic ecology, and seasonal variability. Combined, these studies define water management measures (cut-off drains, dewatering wells, treatment) and mitigation plans required by regulators and lenders.
