From Acid to Nutrients: Fertilizer Manufacturing of DAP, MAP, and TSP

Using phosphoric acid from step 2 (Purification and concentration), fertilizer plants produce solid phosphate fertilizers with different N:P ratios. The major products are DAP (diammonium phosphate, (NH₄)₂HPO₄), MAP (monoammonium phosphate, NH₄H₂PO₄) and TSP (triple superphosphate, Ca(H₂PO₄)₂·H₂O). These differ by whether ammonia is added and how the acid reacts with rock.

DAP (18-46-0):

Production begins by reacting hot purified phosphoric acid (about 75–85% P₂O₅) with gaseous ammonia in a neutralization reactor. The ideal ratio is about 2 mol NH₃ per mol H₃PO₄ (to produce (NH₄)₂HPO₄). The reaction is highly exothermic, producing a hot slurry of 65–70% DAP. Off-gases containing unreacted ammonia and fine dust pass to a scrubber tower, where they are contacted with dilute acid to recover ammonia and capture particulates. The hot DAP slurry is then cooled (often in large coolers), granulated, and dried. Granulation forms roundish pellets, which are then sized by screening. Typical plant steps are: pre-neutralization, ammoniation/granulation, scrubbing (to meet emissions standards), drying and cooling, and screening to final granule size. Throughout, dust is collected via cyclones or baghouses.

MAP (11-52-0):

MAP is made similarly but uses a 1:1 molar ratio of NH₃ to H₃PO₄. This produces NH₄H₂PO₄ (MAP) slurry, which has slightly different pH (more acidic) than DAP. MAP can be produced in a simple pipe reactor or rotary drum, then granulated. One advantage is that MAP requires only half as much ammonia per P₂O₅ compared to DAP. No extra steps beyond those used for DAP granulation are needed, except that MAP granules are usually acid-washed in the scrubber due to their higher acidity. Off-gases are similarly scrubbed with acid to recover ammonia

TSP (0-46-0):

TSP is produced without ammonia. Instead, finely ground phosphate rock is mixed with high-purity phosphoric acid in a pug mill or cone mixer. The acid dissolves the rock, forming mainly mono- and di-calcium phosphate and leaving gypsum. For non-granular TSP, the resulting wet powder is discharged onto a belt or curing area, where it “cures” (reacts slowly) for days to weeks, producing dry, powdery TSP. For granular TSP, the slurry is sprayed onto seed granules (small sand or recycled material) to build up size. The granules are then cured and screened. TSP contains no nitrogen but has ~15% calcium and is strongly acidic (pH of slurry ~2).

Scientific principles:

In DAP/MAP, neutralization reactions occur (acid–base), and granulation relies on nucleation and crystallization as the slurry cools. The pH of the reaction is managed to control particle formation. TSP production is essentially another acidulation step: excess phosphoric acid dissolves rock phosphate, so the same solubility rules apply. Curing allows residual acid to fully react with rock.

Environmental considerations:

Ammonia slip and dust are key concerns. Modern plants use scrubbers: packed towers or venturi scrubbers that use recycled acid to capture ammonia and particulates. Particulate filters capture dust from granulators and dryers. All ammonia in off-gas is recovered to minimize emissions. Wet scrubbers may produce dilute ammonium sulfate, which can be sold or recycled. Energy integration is common: the heat from ammoniation can preheat incoming acid or produce steam. Water use is moderate, limited to cooling.

Infrastructure integration:

The phosphoric acid (PAP plant output) and ammonia feed determine production rates. A local SAP/PAP complex ensures a steady acid supply. A captive Ammonia Plant (not detailed here) typically feeds NH₃. Power and steam from the plant’s cogeneration unit supply granulator heaters, driers, and kilns. Molten Sulfur Units and the SAP indirectly support fertilizer output by providing continuous acid. Gypsum from TSP and DAP (tendency to form small quantities during reaction) may be reused in other processes or stockpiled. The entire fertilizer complex is often integrated so that heat and material (like reclaimed acid) are shared among units.
