Transforming Rock to Acid: Phosphoric Acid Production through Acidulation

Phosphoric acid is produced by acidulating phosphate rock with concentrated sulfuric acid. This wet-process converts tricalcium phosphate in the rock to soluble phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) and precipitates calcium sulfate (gypsum) as a byproduct. The reaction is highly exothermic: for example, apatite (Ca₅(PO₄)₃F) plus sulfuric acid yields H₃PO₄, gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O), and hydrofluoric acid. In practice, ground rock and ~93–98% H₂SO₄ are continuously fed to an acidulation reactor or digestion tower, where heat is managed by cooling. Gypsum crystals form in the slurry and are removed by large filters or centrifuges. The weak (∼25–30% P₂O₅) phosphoric acid solution leaving this step contains soluble impurities and is routed to purification and concentration.

Process steps :

Rock preparation:

Dry, crush and finely grind phosphate ore

Sulfuric acid addition:

Sulfuric acid (from the site SAP) is metered with precise rock feed.

Digestion:

In multi-stage digesters, rock reacts with H₂SO₄ to form H₃PO₄ and gypsum.

Filtration :

The slurry is filtered; gypsum (phosphogypsum) is washed thoroughly (typically >99% acid recovery) and dewatered.

Acid output :

The filtrate (“weak acid”) containing 20–30% H₃PO₄ and soluble impurities (F⁻, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, etc.) is collected.

Scientific principles:

The main reaction is an acid–base type where Ca in rock reacts with sulfate to form insoluble gypsum, freeing the phosphoric acid. Solubility drives gypsum precipitation (CaSO₄ is sparingly soluble) while H₃PO₄ remains in solution. Heat management is critical because the reaction generates heat; higher temperatures increase reaction rates but can also lead to unwanted side reactions or scaling.

Infrastructure integration:

Integrated fertilizer complexes include Molten Sulfur Units to melt elemental sulfur and feed the Sulfuric Acid Plant (SAP), which produces the 93–98% H₂SO₄ for acidulation. Sulfuric acid is stored in large acid tanks (Sulfuric Acid Storage) before use. Desalination/Freshwater Plants supply process water for mixing with acid and washing gypsum. Excess heat from the SAP (from cooling towers or condensers) may be used in a nearby Power Plant or desalination unit. For example, one facility was designed with a 3000 mt/d SAP delivering 98.5% acid via a buffer tank to a 650 mt/d Phosphoric Acid Plant.

Environmental considerations:

The byproduct gypsum (phosphogypsum) is produced in large quantities and often stockpiled; it contains impurities (F, Ra) and must be managed carefully. Wastewater from filtration and cooling is acidic with dissolved phosphate and fluoride; modern plants recycle this water in cooling ponds to reuse water and minimize discharge. Acid mists and volatile HF (from fluorapatite) are scrubbed or absorbed – for example, industrial designs include fluorine-absorption units that capture HF to produce fluosilicic acid. Strict containment and water treatment ensure any overflow or leachate meets environmental standards. Using high-purity acid (93–98%) and automatic controls optimizes efficiency and reduces energy needed for later evaporation. Overall, acidulation is resource- and energy-intensive but indispensable: phosphoric acid “serves as a pivotal intermediate” for downstream fertilizers like DAP and NPK.
