Enhancing Quality: Purification and Concentration of Phosphoric Acid

Raw wet-process phosphoric acid contains dissolved impurities (Fe, Al, Mg, organic carbon, fluoride) from the rock and acid. Purification removes these to meet end-use specifications. Typically, the weak acid (∼25% P₂O₅) is first clarified by allowing fine solids to settle or be filtered out. Then chemical treatments (e.g. lime or ammonia precipitation) and filtration remove metallic impurities. Many plants use filter aids (clays or silicas) and may employ solvent extraction or ion-exchange for heavy metals. For example, purification often involves passing the acid through coagulant filters and treating it to meet purity grades. The result is purified phosphoric acid with drastically lower suspended solids and metal content.

Key steps :

Filtration/Cooling

The hot filtered acid is often cooled to precipitate or separate some salts.

Chemical treatment:

Additives cause impurity salts (e.g. aluminum or iron phosphates) to precipitate.

Second filtration:

Remove the precipitated impurities.

Optional solvent extraction or membrane processes :

Advanced plants may use solvent extraction or nanofiltration to target specific contaminants.

Water recycling :

Waste or condensate waters (rich in P and F) are recycled back into the process to limit discharges.

Scientific principles:

Purification exploits solubility differences: metals and undesirable ions form insoluble compounds at controlled pH or temperature and precipitate out. Fluorides often remain in solution as HF or hexafluorosilicates and require careful handling. The goal is high phosphate recovery with minimal losses.

Concentration :

After purification, the acid is typically still dilute (~25% P₂O₅). It is concentrated by vacuum evaporation or multi-effect evaporators. Here, low-pressure boiling drives off water (often recovered as condensate for process reuse), raising acid strength to 40–55% P₂O₅ or higher. Multiple evaporator stages (feed-forward systems) may be used to improve energy efficiency. The purified acid is thereby concentrated to the desired grade (often ~54% P₂O₅ for fertilizer grade).

Integration :

Concentrated acid often requires heat or vacuum, so it benefits from on-site steam and power. Excess steam from the SAP and power plant can drive evaporators. The Power Plant supplies electricity and steam; heat recovery from acid condensers may be used in desalination or process heating. Cooling towers and condensers tie into the plant’s water circuit.

Environmental considerations:

Evaporation uses substantial heat, so modern plants recover heat via multiple stages to reduce fuel use. Off-gases (water vapor, acid mist) are condensed and treated; the condensate (often acidic) is recycled for scrubbers or dilution water. Purification generates sludge of gypsum or metal phosphates; these sludges are often reused (e.g. blended into fertilizers) or landfilled in containment areas. By concentrating acid, the volume of material shipped is reduced, lowering transport emissions. Any residual effluents are neutralized, and solid wastes (like iron phosphate or calcium fluoride) are managed under hazardous-waste guidelines if needed.
