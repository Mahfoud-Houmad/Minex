Containing the Core: Safe Storage of Phosphoric Acid

Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) is stored in dedicated tank farms built with corrosion-resistant materials. Tanks are typically constructed of 316L stainless steel (extra-low-carbon) or fiber-reinforced plastic (FRP), often with rubber linings or coating (e.g. chlorobutyl). These materials withstand the acid’s corrosiveness. Tank farms include level gauges, pressure-relief vents and closed-loop recirculation systems. All tanks sit inside a dike (bund) – an earthen or concrete containment wall around the tank area – designed to hold at least 110% of the largest tank’s volume. The bunded floor slopes to a sump so that any leaked acid can be pumped out safely. Acid loading points have spill panels and neutralization stations (e.g. with lime), and tank vents are piped to scrubbers or neutralizing towers. Redundant pumps, automated fill alarms, and remote monitoring ensure early leak detection and alarm any overfill. Regular inspections check for corrosion or leaks on tank walls, piping and fittings.

Tank materials :

316L stainless steel with high nickel content is preferred. FRP or high-density polyethylene (HDPE) tanks are also used (per industry guides, phosphoric acid tanks are made of HDPE or FRP to resist corrosion). Carbon steel must always be rubber-lined to survive acid contact.

Containment (diking):

The tank farm is fully bunded. Bund walls are sized for ≥110% of tank volume, and floors are lined (acid-proof) to prevent soil contamination. Sumps collect leaks for treatment.

Temperature control:

Concentrated phosphoric acid has a high freezing point, so tanks may require heat tracing. For example, 75% H₃PO₄ (≈ 54% P₂O₅) can be stored outdoors safely, but 80–85% grades may freeze at ambient temperatures. Low-temperature steam or hot-water circulation loops are installed to keep acid above freezing. Automated temperature and flow controls maintain safe levels.

Safety systems:

Vent gas scrubbers or packed towers neutralize escaping fumes. Flame and vapor detectors monitor the area. Emergency neutralizer feed (lime, soda ash) and spill kits are on hand. Fire suppression systems (water spray with corrosion additives) protect tanks from external fire. Personnel working around the tanks must use acid-resistant PPE and follow strict safety protocols.
