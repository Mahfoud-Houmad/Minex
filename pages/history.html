THE DISCOVERY OF MOROCCO’S WHITE GOLD

1901-1907

Early Geological Observations

French geologist Abel Brives traveled across southern Morocco, particularly in the Chichaoua and Imin Tanout regions. He carefully mapped geological formations and noticed unusual mineral-rich layers that hinted at the presence of phosphates. Although the technology and infrastructure to exploit these deposits did not yet exist, Brives’ surveys provided the first scientific basis for further exploration. His reports sparked interest among mining companies and colonial authorities. This period represents the conceptual birth of Morocco’s phosphate story.

1912

Official Discovery near El Borouj

In 1912, geologists MM. Combelas and Lamolinerie confirmed the first commercially viable phosphate deposits west of Oued Zem, near El Borouj. The discovery came as Morocco entered the French Protectorate era, opening doors for foreign investment in mining. This moment transformed phosphate from a geological curiosity into a strategic industrial resource. The location’s proximity to potential transport routes made it ideal for future exploitation. It laid the groundwork for Khouribga to become the heart of Moroccan phosphate production for decades.

August 20, 1920

Creation of the Office Chérifien (OCP)

By royal decree, the Moroccan state established the OCP to manage, regulate, and develop the phosphate sector. This new entity was charged with turning the promising discoveries of the previous decade into a structured, large-scale industry. With state backing and foreign expertise, OCP began recruiting engineers, building administrative centers, and planning extraction sites. The organization’s creation marked a turning point, giving Morocco an institutional tool to harness its phosphate wealth. OCP would go on to become a global leader in the industry.

March 1921

Launch of Mining at Khouribga

In March 1921, OCP opened its first mining site in Khouribga, with operations managed from nearby Boujniba. The mine’s location was chosen for its high-quality deposits and accessibility to planned railway lines. Early operations were labor-intensive, relying on both Moroccan workers and imported technical expertise. Within months, the site was producing significant quantities of raw phosphate ready for processing. This marked Morocco’s entry into organized, industrial-scale phosphate production, setting the stage for rapid growth in the sector.

1921

First Export via Casablanca

Just months after mining began, the first shipments of Moroccan phosphate left through the port of Casablanca. This achievement was made possible by the rapid construction of transport infrastructure, including a dedicated railway from Khouribga to the coast. Exporting phosphate positioned Morocco as an emerging supplier in the international fertilizer market. These early exports also demonstrated the OCP’s efficiency in integrating mining, logistics, and trade. From this point onward, phosphate became a key pillar of Morocco’s economic strategy.

1931

Opening of Youssoufia Mine

A decade after Khouribga’s debut, OCP expanded by opening the Youssoufia mine, strategically located to diversify production and reduce dependency on a single site. This expansion required new infrastructure, from extraction facilities to worker housing, and it further strengthened Morocco’s role in the global phosphate market. The addition of Youssoufia increased overall output capacity and ensured greater stability in supply. It also marked the beginning of Morocco’s multi-site mining operations, a model still in use today.

THE INDUSTRIAL JOURNEY OF MOROCCAN PHOSPHATE

March 1921

Start of Industrial Mining in Khouribga

After initial phosphate discovery, large-scale industrial mining began in Khouribga with the establishment of basic extraction and transport infrastructure. This marked the transition from small exploratory work to a full-fledged mining industry. Early mechanization and rail connections to Casablanca port allowed Morocco to enter the international phosphate market. The site rapidly grew into the cornerstone of national phosphate production.

1931

Expansion to Youssoufia and New Mining Sites

To meet increasing demand, OCP expanded operations to the Youssoufia mine, diversifying production locations. This period saw investment in new infrastructure, including worker housing, equipment, and transport networks. These expansions significantly increased output capacity and reinforced Morocco’s position as a leading phosphate exporter.

June 1965

Launch of Safi Processing Plant

The Safi facility was opened to begin processing raw phosphate into phosphoric acid and other intermediates. This industrial step marked Morocco’s entry into phosphate beneficiation, adding value to raw materials before export. It was a major technological advancement that boosted the country’s industrial capabilities and export revenues.

1982

Establishment of Jorf Lasfar Fertilizer Complex

OCP launched the Jorf Lasfar plant, the largest fertilizer complex in Africa, capable of producing phosphate-based fertilizers at scale. This project represented a major vertical integration step—from mining raw phosphate to manufacturing finished fertilizer products—positioning Morocco as a global supplier.

1990s

Modernization and Mechanization

Throughout the 1990s, OCP invested heavily in modern mining machinery, automation, and improved extraction techniques. This modernization enhanced productivity, reduced labor intensity, and minimized environmental impact. The adoption of computer-aided mining operations marked a technological leap for Moroccan phosphate exploitation.

2010s–Present

Digital Transformation and Sustainability Focus

In recent years, OCP has embraced digital technologies—such as real-time monitoring, IoT devices, and data analytics—to optimize operations. Alongside modernization, strong commitments to environmental sustainability have been integrated, including water recycling, emission reduction, and waste management practices. This era reflects OCP’s drive toward responsible and innovative mining.

OCP: THE BACKBONE OF MOROCCO'S PHOSPHATE INDUSTRY

Africa can lead the way in building its own model of sustainable agriculture. At OCP, we are committed to harnessing innovation, renewable energy, and tailored fertilization solutions to empower farmers—not just in Morocco, but across the globe.

– Mostafa Terrab, Chairman & CEO of OCP Group

Founded in 1920 as the Office Chérifien des Phosphates, OCP has grown from operating a single mine in Khouribga to becoming a global leader in the phosphate industry. Over the decades, the company expanded its mining operations to Youssoufia (1931) and Benguerir (1976), while also developing world-class chemical processing hubs in Safi (1965) and Jorf Lasfar (1984). Today, OCP operates across five continents, managing every stage of the phosphate value chain — from extraction and transformation to logistics, education, and community development. Owned by the Moroccan Government and Banque Populaire du Maroc since 2008, OCP continues to drive economic growth while committing to environmental stewardship and fostering innovation in partnership with local and global stakeholders.

KEY ROLES

EXTRACTION LEADER

OCP operates the largest phosphate reserves in the world, managing major mining sites like Khouribga, Gantour, and Bou Craa. The company ensures efficient extraction while preserving resources.

GLOBAL SUPPLIER

Through strategic partnerships and extensive logistics infrastructure, OCP delivers phosphate and fertilizers to over 160 countries, meeting global food security needs.

VALUE CHAIN INNOVATOR

Beyond mining, OCP transforms raw phosphate into phosphoric acid, fertilizers, and other products, adding significant economic value.

ECONOMIC CONTRIBUTOR

OCP is Morocco’s largest exporter, generating billions in revenue and contributing significantly to GDP and job creation.

SUSTAINABILITY CHAMPION

The group leads environmental programs for water recycling, renewable energy integration, and carbon footprint reduction.
